Tours

Connemara

From its Atlantic coastline, through the mountain trails of Connemara National Park, this region of Ireland has breathtaking landscapes to get lost in. Start with a visit to the Connemara Heritage and History centre, or view the exhibition at the visitor centre before choosing a trail through the rolling Irish mountains. If you’d rather stay off your feet, Connemara is home of the famous Connemara pony, and there are numerous riding schools offering treks int he area.

This is one of the few places left where the Gaelic language is spoken fluently, and in the towns spotted along the coastline, you can enjoy traditional Irish music in many of the venues.

Cliffs of Mohar

No trip to the west of Ireland is complete without visiting the famous cliffs of Moher. This dramatic 14 kilometre stretch of the Atlantic coastline has inspired generations and appears as a backdrop to many Hollywood films including The Princess Bride and Harry Potter. You can view the cliffs from the sea by ferry, from the visitor centre, or you can follow the 18 kilometre cliff walk.

The cliffs stand at the edge of the Burren in county Clare, a wholly unique landscape hosting ancient stone aged structures and vast cave networks. This region plays host to many of Ireland native flora and fauna, as the unique climate supports both arctic and Mediterranean plants, attracting scientists and enthusiasts from around the world.

The Aran Islands

Take a trip to Inish Mór, Inish Meáin and Inish Oír, three Islands known as the Isls of saints and scholars. Located to the west beyond Galway Bay, they retain some the most authentically traditional Irish culture to be found. You can take a day trip from Galway City or really soak in the culture with a longer stay in b&bs hotels or various camping locations on the islands.

The sites Include scenic cottages, wild landscapes and ancient churches and cliff top ruins. The most famous of these is Dun Aonghasa, a three thousand year old fort perched on a cliff 100 meters over the sea.

Galway City

The capital in the west, Galway city originated near the seat of the King of Connacht, and grew up around a trading port on the mouth of the river Corrib. Now known as the cultural capital of Ireland, Galway is a bustling city of arts and music.

Eyre square is the perfect place to start exploring. Adjacent to shopping streets and the main bus and railway services to the city, this open park hosts markets and festivals regularly, a tradition dating back to medieval times. To further soak in the history of the city, discover the Spanish arch that remain from the front walls of the old Galway city, the Galway city museum and the stunning Galway Cathedral on the banks of the river Corrib.
